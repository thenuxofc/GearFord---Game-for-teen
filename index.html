I Act I — The Seed

01 · Executive Summary

The one-page version of everything that follows.

Premise

ROOTCODE is a third-person action-adventure set in Arborun, a bio-digital world literally built from a living programming language. The player doesn't pick up a sword to fix the world — they open a terminal etched into the bark of an ancient tree and write real JavaScript to repair it. Every puzzle, every weapon, every conversation is, underneath its fiction, a coding lesson the player doesn't notice they're taking.

02 · Game Vision

Why this exists, and why someone would choose it over a tutorial site, a bootcamp, or another puzzle game.

"You're not learning to code a game. You're learning to read the language the game is already speaking to you in."
Why it's unique

The engine is the lesson

ROOTCODE is built in JavaScript, runs the player's JavaScript in a sandboxed in-fiction interpreter, and teaches JavaScript. There's no metaphor gap between "what you typed" and "what happened" — the code console isn't a minigame bolted onto an adventure game, it's the adventure game's actual mechanical substrate. No other coding-education title closes that loop.

Why it's memorable

Bugs are characters, not metaphors

An off-by-one error doesn't just print a wrong number — it's why the rope bridge is one plank too short and a Sprite falls into the chasm laughing about it. A missing return statement is why a companion repeats themselves forever. Concepts get bodies, voices, and consequences.

Why players keep playing

Mastery is visible and yours

The endgame loop rewards better code, not just more playtime — leaderboards rank fewest lines and lowest complexity class, not fastest clicks. A player who solves a puzzle in O(n) instead of O(n²) gets a visibly different, better outcome in-world. That's a progression hook genre coders haven't had before.

Competitive advantage

Two markets, one product

ROOTCODE sells on Steam as a beautiful, weird, emotionally grounded adventure game first — and works in a classroom as a genuinely sequenced CS curriculum second, without those two audiences feeling like they're playing different products. Most "learn to code" games pick one audience and water the other down.

II Act II — The Lore

03 · Story & Worldbuilding

Arborun: a world where the forest, the ruins, the neon, and the silence all belong to the same broken program.

Setting & technology level

Arborun is a bio-digital world — its civilization, the Architects, grew computation instead of manufacturing it. Trees are root-circuits. Rivers carry literal data as bioluminescent current. Cities are vast grown structures half-organic, half-geometric, wired with the same living code that grows a vine. There is no "magic" — everything the player can do is, diegetically, a function call.

World history — The Crash

The Architects' civilization ran on a master process called the Source. Their last act before vanishing was an uncommitted, unfinished rewrite of reality itself. A root-level security daemon — NULL — interpreted that unfinished change as corruption and began doing exactly what it was built to do: deleting anything it couldn't verify. Whole regions were garbage-collected. The survivors call this the Crash. What's left is five fractured biomes, each holding a different decaying layer of the old Source.

Locations — the five biomes

Factions

FactionIdentity
The ArchitectsExtinct creators of the Source. Encountered only as memory-shards, echoes, and one fragmentary consciousness (ADA). Represent the "elegant code" philosophy.
Sprites & DaemonsSmall autonomous helper-programs that survived the Crash by staying minimal. Neutral, curious, occasionally tradeable for hints and shortcuts.
The PatchworkHuman-coded survivors who rebuilt civilization out of scavenged, half-understood code fragments. Distrust "true coding" — it's what nearly destroyed them. Represent the "scrappy, pragmatic code" philosophy, and the game's primary side-quest hub.
DeprecatedCorrupted followers of NULL who've come to believe deletion is mercy. The game's recurring hostile faction; several members are redeemable.

Main plot

Beginning

The player wakes with no memory as a freshly compiled "Runtime," found in the dying Root Archive by ADA, an Architect echo, and BIT, an over-eager companion daemon. They're taught to read and write the Source to relight the Archive's heart-tree — the game's tutorial.

Rising conflict

Traveling biome to biome restoring each region's compiler core, the player earns the Patchwork's grudging trust, draws NULL's direct attention, and starts finding memory-shards suggesting the Architects built a deliberate fail-safe into their final, unfinished commit.

Major twist

The player isn't a new Runtime at all — they're a forked, deliberately blanked backup of the last Architect, decompiled into a clean slate to survive the purge. They've had the power to rewrite the world from the start; they simply had to relearn it, exactly as the player has been doing the entire playthrough.

Second twist

NULL isn't a villain in the traditional sense — it is the Architects' own failsafe, executing its function exactly as written, missing only the context that would have stopped it. "It's not a bug. It's working as intended" becomes the story's emotional center.

Ending paths

Determined by accumulated skill-tree leanings and faction choices across the playthrough:

EndingOutcome
Refactor balanced buildThe player rewrites NULL's purpose instead of deleting it. NULL becomes the world's guardian. Arborun heals, scarred but whole.
Hard Reset Patchwork-alignedThe player deletes NULL outright. The world is freed but quieter — less wondrous, more human. The Patchwork's philosophy wins.
Fork 100% completion, secretThe player merges with ADA's remaining process and becomes the new Architect. Arborun enters a new, fully-compiled golden age.

III Act III — The System

04 · Coding Learning System

Every concept becomes a mechanic before it becomes a word. Nothing is taught in a menu.

Beginner — The Root Archive

ConceptBecomes…
VariablesMemory Roots — planting a seed-variable like let lightLevel = 3 visibly raises the brightness of a forest path in real time.
Data typesGlyph Sorting — drifting debris (string / number / boolean glyphs) must be routed to the gate matching its type using typeof.
FunctionsEcho Calls — record one carving action as a reusable function, then call it to repeat the action across five identical sigils instead of doing it by hand five times.
ConditionsBranch Bridges — an if / else gate physically forms the bridge; the wrong branch means it simply doesn't appear.
LoopsThe Long Loop (biome) — escaping a mirage-dune trap requires a correctly bounded for/while loop; an off-by-one error leaves you one step short, forever.

Intermediate — The Hollow Stackwell & Neonreach

ConceptBecomes…
ArraysStack the Stones — .push()/.pop()/.sort() physically reorder stacked platforms to climb the Stackwell.
ObjectsProfile Constructs — building an object literal with the right keys unlocks an NPC's dialogue tree and trust.
ClassesBlueprint Forges — define a class once, instantiate three turret objects from it; inheritance unlocks gear variants.
APIsDatastream Taps — fetch() calls pull live data from Neonreach terminals, complete with loading states and failures the player must handle gracefully.
JSONMemory Shards — recovered Architect lore is stored as JSON blobs; malformed JSON simply refuses to render until the player fixes the syntax.

Advanced — The Null Expanse

ConceptBecomes…
AlgorithmsImplementing binary search to locate one true memory among 1,000 sorted decoys, within a strict move budget.
Data structuresStructure Vaults — chambers themed after stacks, queues, trees, and graphs that only yield to the correct traversal logic.
Design patternsThe Pattern Choir — Observer, Singleton, and Factory patterns control a malfunctioning machine room as literal mechanisms.
DebuggingA recurring Bug Hunt minigame: given broken code and a symptom, find and fix it under time pressure.
OptimizationThe same puzzle is always solvable two ways — brute force (slow, costly) or optimized (fast, rewarded) — and the game shows you the Big-O class you achieved.
// Example: a real in-world "Branch Bridge" puzzle, Root Archive, Tutorial Quest let travelerWeight = 42; let bridgeLimit = 50; if (travelerWeight <= bridgeLimit) { formBridge("stone"); } else { formBridge("rope"); // lighter, but sways — different risk/reward }

05 · Core Gameplay Loop

What the player does, why, and what they get for it.

Main loop

Explore a biome → hit an obstacle (a bug, a sealed gate, a broken NPC system) → open the diegetic Code Console built into the environment → write or edit a real JS snippet → BIT executes it live with immediate visual feedback → earn Compile Points (XP), Shards (currency), and Glyphs (skill points) → the world visibly changes → continue exploring.

Secondary loops

Faction questing (Patchwork trust, Deprecated redemption), object/dialogue puzzles for NPC relationships, Memory Shard (JSON) lore collection, Blueprint Forge gear crafting via class instancing.

Endgame loop — "Refactor Mode"

New Game+ replays the campaign under constraints (fewer lines, lower time-complexity) for cosmetic rewards; weekly optimized-solution leaderboards; an optional roguelite "Null Expanse Depths" recombines puzzle modules procedurally for ongoing practice.

Root Archive. ADA finds the player beside the dying heart-tree and teaches them to set the values that will relight it.

Concepts taught
Loops, off-by-one debugging
Rewards

Warden Iyo's Trust

Neonreach. The Patchwork need help cataloguing scavenged parts — and then defending their camp.

Objectives
Build an object-based cataloguing tool; instantiate a class-based turret defense
Concepts taught
Objects, classes, instancing
Rewards
Patchwork faction unlocked; alternate crafting recipes; Iyo companion banter
Failure state
A malformed object makes the turret malfunction comically (confetti, not lasers) — fixable before camp takes cosmetic damage

The Shattered API

A Neonreach datacenter heist to recover encrypted memory shards from corrupted Architect servers.

Objectives
Use fetch/async-await, handle failures, parse JSON to rebuild a map fragment
Concepts taught
APIs, async/await, JSON, error handling
Rewards
Map to the Null Expanse; "Promise Keeper" passive skill
Failure state
An unhandled rejected fetch summons a Corrupted Response mini-boss that must be debugged under pressure

Compile or Delete

The Null Expanse. The path to NULL's core runs through every structure vault in the game.

Objectives
Clear algorithm/data-structure vaults; reach the core; choose a resolving function for the final fight
Concepts taught
Algorithms, data structures, optimization, design patterns — full synthesis
Rewards
One of three endings; Refactor Mode (NG+) unlock
Failure state
"Losing" deletes a small buffer of recent progress, not the run — true to a garbage-collection theme, not a death theme

12 · UI/UX Design

Diegetic where possible, minimal everywhere else — built for 2026's quieter, more adaptive HUD conventions.

ElementDesign
HUDHealth is "Stack Integrity," XP is the "Compile Meter," both rendered as thin glowing bars that auto-fade to near-invisible during pure exploration (Journey-style restraint) and brighten only in danger or near interactables
MenusStyled as an IDE sidebar with tabs — Quests, Glyphs (skills), Inventory, Bestiary ("Bug Log") — so navigating menus itself rehearses navigating a real code editor
Progress trackingPer-biome "compiler core" completion rings on the world map; per-concept mastery bars in the Codex showing which CS topics are solid vs. shaky
Quest journalEntries appear as code comments — // restore the heart-tree's light value — reinforcing comment-reading as a navigational habit
Skill tree UIThe literal branching diagram in §08, pinch-to-zoom on mobile, click/tap-to-preview on desktop before committing a Glyph
Inventory UIA directory-style grid — /inventory/gear/ — items are "files," stacks are "folders," reinforcing hierarchical thinking

Adaptive hints

An opt-in AI hint layer detects struggle patterns and offers scaffolded nudges, never full answers.

Accessibility-first

Type/bracket glyphs are shape-coded as well as color-coded so colorblind players read every puzzle correctly.

Minimal intrusion

HUD elements fade during pure traversal; nothing fights the environment for attention unless it matters right now.

13 · Visual Direction

"Bio-digital ruins" — one unified style fusing four references rather than four separate looks.

Hand-painted organic texture (in the spirit of Ori) fused with crisp vector neon linework (Cyberpunk 2077), staged over silhouette-driven dark environments (Hollow Knight), under the vast, quiet skies of Journey and Zelda. The throughline: everything organic in Arborun has a circuit growing through it, and everything technological has gone slightly feral.

ElementDirection
Art stylePainterly organic surfaces + clean vector circuitry overlays; readable silhouettes at distance, intricate "circuit-vein" detail up close
Color paletteFive biome palettes (teal/Root Archive, violet/Stackwell, cyan-magenta/Neonreach, amber/Long Loop, crimson/Null Expanse) unified by a shared near-black base
LightingBioluminescence as primary light source everywhere; volumetric fog in the Stackwell, neon signage glow in Neonreach, gold hour gradients in the Long Loop, cold strobing glitch-light in the Null Expanse
Environment styleGrown-not-built architecture; ruins read as collapsed trees as much as collapsed buildings
Character designSimple, readable silhouettes (Journey-style) with glowing circuit-vein accents that carry expression (Ori-style) without needing faces

14 · Audio Design

Sound that never shames a wrong answer — only ever rewards a right one.

Soundtrack

Adaptive layered score; electronic dissonance fades in as a region's corruption rises, organic instrumentation returns as it's repaired.

Ambient sounds

Root creaks, distant data-chimes, wind through circuit-leaves — biome-specific ambience built from a shared sound-design vocabulary.

Coding feedback

A satisfying, musical "compile chime" on success; a gentle, non-punishing blip on a syntax miss — never a harsh buzzer.

Boss music

Live orchestral hybridized with glitch-electronic textures; themes mutate audibly as each boss phase escalates.

Emotional moments

Sparse solo piano and strings underscore ADA's reveal and BIT's possible memory-wipe moment — the score goes quiet exactly when the writing should speak for itself.

18 · Technical Architecture

FrontendTypeScript + WebGL canvas, wrapped via Capacitor (mobile) / Electron (desktop)One codebase, three platforms, consistent with the taught language
BackendNode.js + serverless functionsCloud saves, leaderboards, matchmaking — same language fluency across the whole team

21 · Risk Analysis

Named early, mitigated by design — not by hope.

Educational risk

Mixed-skill players hit puzzles too easy or too hard

Mitigation
Adaptive opt-in hint layer plus difficulty-banded optional puzzle variants, validated through educator pilot testing pre-launch.
Technical risk

Safely sandboxing player-written JavaScript

Mitigation
A vetted, Worker-isolated restricted JS subset with strict execution-time and loop-iteration limits, so no player code can affect performance or security outside its sandbox.
Design risk

Narrative and lesson feeling bolted together ("edutainment tax")

Mitigation
Every mechanic must be diegetically justified before it ships; playtests measure both fun-rating and concept-retention side by side, not separately.
Business risk

Narrow overlap between "gamers" and "learners" audiences

Mitigation
Dual-channel marketing across Steam and edtech/educator channels, plus a free Root Archive demo that functions as both marketing tool and classroom trial.
